# Structure

Same broad strokes as the original Taisei proposal: UHPC megacolumns plus CFRP tendons, or a bundled cluster of sub-towers under one aerodynamic shroud. Skyplates every ~500 m double as event-floor "ground levels" with full programming each.

The park framing actually relaxes the structural problem in one important way. Residential floors carry continuous live load — furniture, water, people, partition walls — and demand uniform vibration limits across every level. Event floors only need to carry their nominal occupancy during operating hours, can be spaced further apart, and can tolerate the kind of low-frequency sway that would make a permanent resident seasick but is unremarkable to a climber on belay or a runner on a stair course. That margin can be spent on slenderness, redundancy, or simply less material.

UHPC handles the compressive load in the lower two-thirds of the tower where dead weight dominates; CFRP tendons take over where weight-to-stiffness matters more, principally above 2 km where the cone narrows and lateral wind load drives the design. The bundled-sub-tower variant trades some plan efficiency for a much better failure mode: a single sub-tower can be taken offline for inspection or repair without closing the whole structure.

## Foundation

TLP-style sea-base, moored to Tokyo Bay bedrock under tension. Tsunami-resistant by mass; doubles as the building's tuned-mass damper.

The 6 km-wide base is not just a footprint — it's a buoyant displacement vessel that pre-tensions the whole assembly downward against the seabed anchors. This is borrowed directly from deepwater oil-platform practice, where TLPs have been holding multi-thousand-tonne topsides in place against North Sea and Gulf of Mexico storms for decades. The novelty here is scale and the requirement that the same structure also functions as a public space at sea level: marina, wave pool, ferry terminal, and the structural ground floor of everything above. Anchor maintenance and corrosion monitoring become permanent operational disciplines on the order of dry-dock cycles for a capital ship.
