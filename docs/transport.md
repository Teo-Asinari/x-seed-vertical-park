# Vertical transport

Three-tier: ropeless maglev express between altitude bands, local cable elevators within bands, escalators / stairs within ~3 floors. Stairwells designed as competition-ready running courses, not just emergency egress.

Conventional cable elevators do not scale to 4 km. The cable's own weight becomes the dominant load somewhere around 600–800 m, which is why every existing supertall building uses a sky-lobby transfer scheme rather than a single run. Ropeless maglev — already prototyped for sub-kilometre buildings — removes the cable entirely, allows multiple cars to share a single shaft, and permits horizontal transfer between shafts at sky-lobby levels. For a 4 km structure it isn't a luxury, it's the only architecture that closes.

The three-tier split exists because the trip-time distribution is bimodal. Most visitors move within a single band for most of their visit (locker → court → cafe → locker). A smaller number make one or two long inter-band hops per visit (sea base → summit). Optimizing one network for both produces a worst-of-both-worlds result; splitting them lets the express maglevs run with high-speed profiles and infrequent stops while the local cable elevators behave like a normal mid-rise building's vertical traffic.

Stairs deserve special attention. The vertical marathon premise depends on stairs that are actually runnable end-to-end — consistent rise/run, non-slip, with hydration and aid stations integrated at ~500 m intervals. Treating stairs as competition infrastructure rather than code-mandated egress also raises the design standard for normal-day use: better light, better air, better signage. It's the same conversion that turned utility bike paths into desirable cycling networks.

Throughput is the binding constraint on daily attendance. Even with high-speed maglev, a 4 km tower's vertical transport almost certainly caps daily attendance below typical theme-park norms. That cap is a programming input — it shapes ticket pricing, residency-vs-day-visitor mix, and event scheduling — not just an operational nuisance.
