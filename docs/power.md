# Power

~200–500 MW continuous (vs. ~5 GW residential). Achievable with rooftop PV, embedded vertical-axis wind in the upper aerodynamic shroud, tidal at the base, and pumped seawater storage. Possibly no nuclear required.

The order-of-magnitude drop in demand is the hidden dividend of the park framing. A residential tower runs HVAC, water heating, cooking, and continuous lighting for a city-sized population around the clock. A park runs lighting and program-specific systems during operating hours, with a much smaller overnight base load for security, maintenance, and any in-residence athletes. The peak is high but the duty cycle is much lower, which favours storage-heavy generation mixes that would not pencil out for a residential load.

Vertical-axis wind turbines are the right choice for the upper shroud for two reasons: they don't care about wind direction (critical at altitudes where wind shear can rotate the apparent flow within seconds), and their rotor mass distribution plays nicely with a structural shroud that is already managing torsional load. Integrating them into the cladding rather than mounting them as discrete machines also avoids the visual and acoustic problems that have hurt conventional rooftop wind on tall buildings. Power output per turbine is modest, but the available surface area at altitude is enormous, and the wind resource above 2 km is dramatically better than at street level.

Pumped seawater storage uses the tower itself as the head. Lifting seawater from the base to a reservoir at, say, 2 km gives a hydraulic head no terrestrial pumped-storage facility can match — the same kilowatt-hour of stored energy occupies far less reservoir volume. The reservoir doubles as fire-suppression supply and as ballast for the structural tuning system. The storage round-trip is what lets intermittent wind and PV cover an event-driven demand curve without grid imports during evening peaks.

Tidal generation at the sea base is comparatively small in absolute terms but useful because its phase is predictable and complementary to solar. The combined mix — PV by day, wind across the diurnal cycle, tidal on the lunar cycle, pumped storage smoothing the gaps — is plausible without a nuclear baseload. That's worth saying out loud because the residential X-Seed's power story essentially required a dedicated reactor, with all the siting and political weight that implies.
